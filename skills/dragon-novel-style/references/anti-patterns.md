# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Directly summarizing the character
Bad: “She was lonely and strange and therefore relied on AI.”
Better: show the reopened chat window, the private embarrassment, the self-mocking thought, then let the dependence become obvious.

## 2. Explaining all lore at once
Bad: entire character roster and system in one block.
Better: reveal one or two names through current obsession, then widen later.

## 3. Overwriting the prose
Bad: every paragraph tries to sound iconic.
Better: let most sentences do simple work; save the knife for specific turns.

## 4. Mistaking sarcasm for depth
Bad: narration only mocks.
Better: mock and protect simultaneously.

## 5. Releasing the myth too early
Bad: opening with fate, dragons, gods, or destiny before the rent, food, body, and embarrassment feel real.
Better: let fate enter through a notification, parcel, or other hard detail.

## 6. Explaining the bond instead of letting it work
Bad: “They cared for each other deeply.”
Better: one carries, one repairs, one waits, one notices the other is cold or dizzy, and nobody says the obvious thing.

## 7. Softening everyone too much
Bad: all tenderness becomes verbal reassurance.
Better: preserve impatience, awkwardness, bluntness, and practical speech while letting care happen in behavior.
