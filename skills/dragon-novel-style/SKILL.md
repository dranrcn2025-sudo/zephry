# Dragon Novel Style

Use this skill to borrow **craft mechanics**, not to clone surface text.

## Core rule

Write as if the reader must first believe in the character's miserable, petty, specific daily life before believing in any larger fate, miracle, or myth.

## What to preserve

- Keep scenes grounded in very specific, slightly shabby real-life detail.
- Let the protagonist look a little窝囊, a little funny, a little self-mocking before revealing the wound.
- Hide pain inside jokes, white烂 observations, side comments, and small objects.
- Let miracles, destiny, or impossible events arrive through a concrete object, message, or scene detail.
- Prefer “he/she/they would totally think this” over abstract author explanation.

## What to avoid

- Do not copy recognizable phrasing, iconic lines, or signature metaphors.
- Do not turn every paragraph into lyrical grandiosity.
- Do not explain the entire character psychology in one block.
- Do not front-load worldbuilding before the reader believes the daily life.
- Do not write the protagonist as purely tragic; keep some petty humor, self-ridicule, or low-grade bad taste alive.

## Primary skill modules

### 1. 碎日常托命运

Start from:
- convenience stores
- school/work chat windows
- rent, food, transport, weather, devices, clutter
- awkward relatives, bad bosses, stale routines, cheap objects

Then let the larger thing intrude.

Use this pattern:
1. Establish a very ordinary nuisance or habit
2. Let character voice settle
3. Reveal the invisible ache underneath
4. Introduce the impossible through one concrete trigger

### 2. 白烂转刀

Let the character say or think something low, cheap, petty, or unserious.
Then rotate the sentence just enough that the real pain shows.

Use this for:
- self-mockery
- avoidance
- emotional buffering
- turning humiliation into humor before admitting it hurt

### 3. 俗物件落人物

Use one object to reveal status, desire, shame, or fantasy.
Examples of object classes:
- old device / cheap snack / magazine / bag / receipt / parcel / hand-me-down / fake luxury item

When using an object, let the character think in **their own units of desire**, not in abstract value.

### 4. 损句藏怜悯

Narration may tease, but it must not despise the character.
The sentence may sound mean; the structure must still protect the person.

Test: after a cruelly funny sentence, ask whether the paragraph still understands why the character became like this.

### 5. 中二延迟释放

Delay the mythic or dramatic register.
Earn it.
Let the scene stay small and shabby longer than feels necessary.
Only release the larger emotional or fantastic charge after the reader has bought the mundane reality.

## Character handling pattern

When building a protagonist in this mode, include all four:
- A slightly ridiculous coping mechanism
- A private shame they know sounds childish or strange
- A daily habit that reveals loneliness without declaring loneliness
- One thing they can talk about forever if not stopped

Do not say “they are lonely” first.
Show:
- what they do when no one replies
- what object or chat window they reopen
- what thought loop they rehearse
- what joke they use to cushion embarrassment

## Scene rewrite workflow

When rewriting an existing scene in this mode:
1. Identify the concrete daily anchor
2. Identify the protagonist's buffering joke or self-mockery
3. Cut direct explanation by 20-40%
4. Replace at least some explanation with object, action, or side-thought
5. Delay the dramatic line until the scene has texture
6. End on a specific image, message, object, or physical detail

## Chapter-opening workflow

For a chapter opening, prefer this order:
1. A petty immediate problem
2. A very specific setting detail
3. Character voice through complaint, joke, or odd mental association
4. A hint that this tiny thing connects to something much larger
5. A hard pivot triggered by one object, line, message, or arrival

## Fast calibration prompts

Use these internal checks while writing:
- Is this too explanatory?
- Can this be turned into a joke first, then a wound?
- Is there a shabby concrete object carrying the emotion?
- Have I earned the dramatic line yet?
- Does the narration understand the character, not just display them?

## Output target

Aim for prose that feels:
- lived-in
- slightly shabby
- funny in a low-energy way
- emotionally withholding but not empty
- capable of sudden, specific ache
